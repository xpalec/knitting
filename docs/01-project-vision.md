# Project vision — European Knitting Encyclopedia

## What we are building

The European Knitting Encyclopedia is a free, open-access multilingual reference website dedicated to the language, techniques, and living traditions of knitting across Europe. It serves as both a practical dictionary for everyday knitters and a cultural archive for educators, researchers, and historians who care about preserving textile heritage.

The encyclopedia brings together knitting phrases, terminology, and traditional techniques from across Europe — starting with Poland, Norway and Scandinavia, Germany and Austria, the United Kingdom and Ireland, and France — and presents them in a structured, searchable, and beautifully organised form. Every entry is anchored to its cultural origin, linked to related terms across languages, and made accessible to speakers of each supported language in their own tongue.

This is not a pattern database or a shopping platform. It is a knowledge resource — the kind of reference that a knitter in Warsaw, a teacher in Oslo, a historian in Edinburgh, and a beginner in Paris can all open and find exactly what they need, in their own language, with the depth they require.

---

## The problem we are solving

Knitting knowledge in Europe is fragmented. Terminology differs not just between countries but between publishers, decades, and regional traditions. A Polish knitter reading a Norwegian pattern encounters terms with no clear equivalent in Polish. A British knitter studying Aran techniques finds that the Irish Gaelic roots of the vocabulary are poorly documented online. A teacher preparing a course on Fair Isle colorwork has no single authoritative source that connects the Shetland tradition to its broader European context.

Existing online resources are either too narrow (single-language glossaries), too shallow (brief definitions with no cultural context), or too commercial (embedded in pattern-selling platforms). No resource exists that treats European knitting terminology as a unified, multilingual, historically grounded body of knowledge worth preserving.

The European Knitting Encyclopedia is built to fill that gap.

---

## Who it is for

The encyclopedia is designed for four overlapping audiences, all of equal importance:

**Beginner knitters** who encounter unfamiliar terms in patterns, tutorials, or classes and need clear, accessible definitions in their own language. They arrive via search, need quick answers, and benefit from skill-level indicators and visual media that show rather than just tell.

**Experienced knitters** who work across languages, follow international designers, or explore traditions outside their own. They need precise terminology, cross-language equivalents, regional variants, and enough technical depth to trust the definitions they find.

**Knitting educators and teachers** who need a reliable, citable reference to support courses, workshops, and educational materials. They benefit from the historical context, the category structure, and the ability to link students directly to specific entries.

**Researchers and historians** interested in textile heritage, cultural history, and the transmission of craft knowledge across Europe. They need origin information, regional attribution, connections between traditions, and a record of how terms have evolved and travelled across borders.

---

## What makes it different

**Multilingual by design, not by translation.** Each language is a first-class citizen of the encyclopedia. Polish users browse and search in Polish, with Polish alphabetical ordering and Polish definitions. German users do the same in German. The canonical English entry exists as a structural anchor, not as the default experience.

**Country and tradition as context, not just metadata.** Entries are connected to their cultural origins — whether that is a specific region of Poland, the Shetland Islands, the Norwegian Selbu tradition, or the French entrelac heritage. Users can browse by tradition as naturally as they browse by technique.

**Community-contributed, editorially verified.** Anyone can submit a new entry or contribute a translation. A dedicated editorial team reviews all submissions before publication. This keeps quality high while allowing the encyclopedia to grow organically through the knowledge of the knitting community itself.

**Rich media as standard.** Every entry can carry photographs, technical diagrams, and short video clips demonstrating the technique. Text definitions alone are not enough for craft knowledge — seeing matters.

**History preserved alongside practice.** The encyclopedia does not separate historical from contemporary content. The origin of a term, the tradition it comes from, and the way it is used in modern patterns all live together in one place.

---

## What it is not

The encyclopedia is not a pattern database. It does not sell patterns, yarn, or tools. It does not require registration to read. It does not contain advertising. It is a pure knowledge resource, free and open to everyone, with no commercial agenda.

---

## Vision statement

To become the definitive multilingual reference for European knitting knowledge — a place where the craft's terminology, techniques, and traditions are documented with the care and depth they deserve, accessible to every knitter regardless of their language, experience, or country of origin.

---

## Guiding principles

**Free and open.** All content is freely accessible to everyone. No paywalls, no registration required to read, no advertising.

**Multilingual first.** The experience in Polish, Norwegian, German, French, and English should feel equally native. Translation is not an afterthought.

**Community and editorial in balance.** The knowledge of the community is the encyclopedia's greatest asset. Editorial oversight ensures it remains trustworthy.

**Depth over breadth.** A well-documented entry with cultural context, media, translations, and related terms is worth more than a hundred shallow definitions.

**Cultural respect.** Traditions are attributed to their origins with care. Regional and national knitting heritages are treated as living culture, not museum pieces.
